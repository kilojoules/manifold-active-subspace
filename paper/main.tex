\begin{abstract}
Deep neural networks progressively transform inputs through a sequence of
representations.  Prior work has characterized these representations in two
independent ways: \emph{geometrically}, by estimating the intrinsic dimension
(ID) of the activation manifold at each layer, and \emph{functionally}, by
identifying which directions in activation space most influence the output
(active subspaces).  We ask whether these two characterizations agree---that
is, whether the directions that dominate the data variance at a given layer
are the same directions along which the network's loss varies most.

We measure alignment via principal angles between (i) the top-$k$ PCA
directions of the activation cloud, where $k$ is set by the TwoNN intrinsic
dimension, and (ii) the top-$k$ eigenvectors of the Average Gradient Outer
Product (AGOP) at the same layer.  On ImageNet-pretrained VGG-16 and
ResNet-18 probed on Imagenette, alignment exceeds the $k/d$ random-subspace
baseline at every layer beyond the first, peaking at the last hidden layers:
$35$--$43\times$ baseline for the VGG-16 FC hidden layers and $7.6\times$
for ResNet-18's final block, before collapsing to ${\approx}2\times$ at the
logits.  Random-init controls sit at $0.8$--$1.3\times$ baseline on the same
measure.  Two controls probe what the alignment is made of: it survives
split-half estimation (PCA and AGOP from disjoint image sets), so it is
population structure rather than per-sample coupling, and it survives
removal of class-mean structure from both objects at $21$--$25\times$
baseline for the VGG FC hidden layers, so it is not reducible to shared
class geometry.  The controls also surface a caveat we quantify: within one
linear map of the logits, gradient residuals are architecturally coupled to
activation residuals (random-init networks show strong within-class
alignment there), so attribution to training is only clean at layers at
least two nonlinearities from the output.

Together, the results suggest that the ID story of representation learning
is incomplete: trained networks do not merely compress representations into
low-dimensional manifolds---they compress them into manifolds whose dominant
directions are \emph{aligned with the task gradient structure}, and this
alignment lives largely in the within-class geometry that neural-collapse
analyses discard.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

A central question in deep learning theory is what makes the internal
representations of trained networks special.  Two geometric traditions have
developed largely in parallel.

The first, exemplified by \citet{ansuini2019intrinsic}, studies the
\emph{intrinsic dimension} (ID) of the activation manifold at each layer: the
minimal number of parameters needed to describe where data points land.
\citet{ansuini2019intrinsic} show that ID follows a characteristic
``hunchback'' profile across layers---rising in early layers and falling
monotonically toward the output---and that the ID of the last hidden layer
predicts generalisation accuracy across architectures.  Crucially, the same
pattern is absent in untrained networks and networks trained on randomised
labels, implicating training and the learning signal specifically.

The second tradition asks which directions in activation space the network's
output actually depends on.  \citet{constantine2015active} introduced
\emph{active subspaces} for scalar-valued functions: the leading eigenspace of
$\AGOP = \E_x[\nabla f(x)\,\nabla f(x)^\top]$, where the expectation
averages the outer product of the gradient over the data distribution.
\citet{cui2020active} extended this to intermediate layers of neural networks,
replacing the input gradient with the gradient of the loss with respect to
intermediate activations.  Recent work by \citet{beaglehole2024agop} connects
the AGOP directly to neural collapse and to the Neural Feature Ansatz
(NFA)~\citep{beaglehole2024nfa}, showing that learned weight matrices align with the
AGOP of their layer.

Neither tradition asks the natural cross-question: \emph{do the directions
that span the data manifold coincide with the directions that drive the
output?}  These could in principle be orthogonal.  A representation might be
geometrically low-dimensional yet have that low-dimensional structure be
irrelevant to the loss; or it might have a low-dimensional active subspace
that is perfectly sufficient for classification even though the data cloud
fills a higher-dimensional space.

The present work provides a direct empirical answer.  We measure alignment as
the mean squared cosine of principal angles between the top-$k$ PCA subspace
of the activation cloud and the top-$k$ AGOP eigenvectors, where $k$ is set
by the TwoNN intrinsic dimension.  For two generic $k$-dimensional subspaces
in $\R^d$ the expected alignment is $k/d$; we normalise by this random
baseline to obtain a dimensionless ratio.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A simple, tractable measure of \emph{manifold-gradient alignment} at
  each layer of a pretrained CNN---one forward-backward pass per batch plus
  standard linear algebra---together with a suite of controls (split-half,
  within-class, crossed, centred-AGOP, $k$-sweep) that separate population
  alignment from sample coupling and class structure.

\item The empirical finding that trained networks align data-variance and
  gradient subspaces above chance at every layer beyond the first
  ($35$--$43\times$ baseline at VGG-16's FC hidden layers, up to
  $7.6\times$ at ResNet-18's final block), that the logits layer collapses
  to ${\approx}2\times$, that random-init controls sit at
  $0.8$--$1.3\times$, and---centrally---that the alignment survives removal
  of class-mean structure: most of it lives in within-class geometry.

\item The identification and mechanism of an architectural confound: within
  one linear map of the logits, gradient residuals are a fixed
  near-isotropic linear image of activation residuals (exact at uniform
  softmax), producing strong within-class ``alignment'' even at random
  init---and, through ResNet's residual stream, propagating deep into the
  network.  Any study of gradient-representation alignment near the output
  must control for this.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Intrinsic Dimension via TwoNN}
\label{sec:twonn}

Given $N$ points $\{x_i\}_{i=1}^N$ in $\R^d$, the \emph{intrinsic dimension}
is the minimal $k \le d$ such that the data approximately lies on a
$k$-dimensional manifold.  \citet{facco2017estimating} propose the TwoNN
estimator, which exploits the ratio $\mu_i = r_i^{(2)}/r_i^{(1)}$ of each
point's second- to first-nearest-neighbour distance.  Under the assumption
that the data density is locally constant on the scale of $r_i^{(2)}$, $\mu$
follows a Pareto distribution with shape parameter $d$ (density
$d\,\mu^{-(d+1)}$ on $[1,\infty)$), whose CDF is $F(\mu) = 1 - \mu^{-d}$.
Following the original procedure, we estimate $d$ by fitting
\begin{equation}
-\log\bigl(1 - F_{\mathrm{emp}}(\mu)\bigr) = \hat{d}\,\log \mu
\label{eq:twonn}
\end{equation}
with a straight line through the origin on the empirical CDF, after
discarding the 10\% largest ratios as prescribed by
\citet{facco2017estimating}.  We cross-check with the closed-form
bias-corrected estimator $\hat d = (N{-}1)/\sum_i \log\mu_i$; the two agree
within error bars at every layer.  TwoNN handles curved, non-uniformly
sampled manifolds, properties that disqualify PCA-based linear ID estimates
\citep{ansuini2019intrinsic}.  A known caveat is finite-sample
underestimation when $\hat d \gtrsim 20$ at moderate $N$; we quantify this on
synthetic data and account for it in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{Active Subspaces and the AGOP}
\label{sec:agop}

For a differentiable function $f:\R^d\to\R$, the \emph{active subspace}
\citep{constantine2015active} is the leading eigenspace of
\begin{equation}
\AGOP = \E_x\!\left[\nabla f(x)\,\nabla f(x)^\top\right] \in \R^{d\times d}.
\label{eq:agop}
\end{equation}
Eigenvectors of $\AGOP$ with large eigenvalues span directions along which $f$
varies most on average; the remaining directions are approximately inactive.

\citet{cui2020active} apply active-subspace analysis to neural networks and
show that the number of \emph{active neurons} at intermediate layers is far
smaller than the layer width.  In the same spirit, we define the layer-$\ell$
AGOP as $\AGOP_\ell =
\E_x[\nabla_{a_\ell}\mathcal{L}\,(\nabla_{a_\ell}\mathcal{L})^\top]$, where
$a_\ell(x)$ are the activations at layer $\ell$ and $\mathcal{L}$ is the
cross-entropy loss; we compute it from per-sample gradients obtained by one
backward pass per batch (retaining gradients on the intermediate activation
tensors).

\subsection{Principal Angles Between Subspaces}
\label{sec:angles}

Given orthonormal bases $U\in\R^{d\times p}$ and $V\in\R^{d\times q}$, the
$\min(p,q)$ principal angles $\theta_1\le\cdots\le\theta_{\min(p,q)}$ satisfy
$\cos\theta_j = \sigma_j(U^\top V)$, the $j$-th singular value of the
cross-Gram matrix \citep{bjorck1973numerical}.  We summarise alignment with the
\emph{mean squared cosine}:
\begin{equation}
\kcos = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^{k}\cos^2\!\theta_j \in [0,1],
\label{eq:alignment}
\end{equation}
where $k = \min(p,q)$.  For two independent uniformly random $k$-dimensional
subspaces in $\R^d$, $\E[\kcos] = k/d$: writing $\kcos =
\frac1k\operatorname{tr}(U^\top V V^\top U)$ and using
$\E[VV^\top] = \frac{k}{d}I_d$ for a Haar-random subspace gives
$\E[\kcos] = \frac1k \cdot \frac{k}{d}\operatorname{tr}(U^\top U) = k/d$.
We verified this empirically at the $(k,d)$ pairs used in our experiments.
We report $\text{ratio} = \kcos/(k/d)$ throughout.  Principal angles are the
natural metric here: for Gaussian-mixture classifiers, misclassification
probability is governed by the principal angles between signal and model
subspaces \citep{huang2016principal}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Intrinsic dimension of representations.}
\citet{ansuini2019intrinsic} use TwoNN to map ID across layers of fourteen
ImageNet-trained CNNs, finding the hunchback profile and a strong Pearson
correlation ($r=0.94$) between the training-set ID of the last hidden layer
and test-set top-5 performance.  They show empirically that PCA-based linear
dimension estimates are roughly an order of magnitude larger than TwoNN
estimates, evidence that the manifolds are curved.
\citet{ma2018dimensionality} connect local ID to robustness against adversarial
examples.  \citet{yadav2026geometry} report that the effective dimension of
output representations predicts ImageNet accuracy across 52 pretrained models
(partial correlation $r=0.75$).

\paragraph{Neural Collapse.}
\citet{papyan2020prevalence} discover that, in the terminal phase of training,
within-class variability collapses (NC1) and class-mean vectors converge to a
simplex ETF (NC2).  \citet{zhu2021geometric} prove analytically under the
unconstrained features model that, at the global minimiser with balanced data
and weight decay, the classifier satisfies $W = \alpha M^\top$ (NC3,
\emph{self-duality}), so the row space of $W$ equals the span of the centred
class means.  Both works restrict their analysis to the last-layer activations and
classifier; \citet{parker2023intermediate} confirm that extending NC to
intermediate layers is an active, open research direction.  Importantly, NC3 is
a relationship between two linear objects (weight vectors and class-mean vectors)
and does \emph{not} directly imply alignment between the data manifold tangent
space and the AGOP active subspace, since it makes no claim about within-class
variability or gradient structure at non-convergence.  Our experiments directly
measure this gap across all layers.

\paragraph{AGOP and the Neural Feature Ansatz.}
The Neural Feature Ansatz (NFA) of \citet{radhakrishnan2024mechanism} posits
that trained weight matrices align with the AGOP of their layer:
$\rho(W_\ell^\top W_\ell, \AGOP_\ell)$ is empirically high across
architectures.  \citet{beaglehole2024agop} show that AGOP projection is the
operative mechanism behind deep neural collapse: in Deep Random Feature
Models, within-class variability collapse improves monotonically with AGOP
projection and worsens without it.  \citet{beaglehole2024nfa} analyse
\emph{why} the NFA holds, reformulating it exactly as alignment between
weight matrices and the pre-activation tangent kernel; the NFA remains an
empirically supported ansatz rather than a proven property of general
nonlinear networks.

\paragraph{Geometry of overparameterisation.}
The lottery ticket hypothesis \citep{frankle2019lottery} proposes that large
networks succeed because they contain sparse winning subnetworks.
\citet{martinelli2026escape} argue that overparameterisation improves
optimisation by adding \emph{escape dimensions}: directions in the loss
landscape that convert sharp local minima into traversable saddle points, making
good minima more prevalent.  Our AGOP effective-rank results speak to this:
trained networks have lower AGOP rank at the output than at intermediate layers,
consistent with the loss landscape being genuinely low-dimensional near
convergence.

\paragraph{Gradient-weighted class activation maps.}
\citet{selvaraju2017gradcam} show that the spatial average of the gradient of
a class score with respect to a convolutional feature map identifies
class-discriminative regions.  Our AGOP computation for convolutional layers
applies the same spatial averaging to obtain a channel-wise gradient, then
forms its outer product.  This quantity is exactly the sensitivity of the
loss to spatially-uniform per-channel perturbations, and coincides (up to
scale) with the gradient of the GAP representation whenever downstream
operations depend on the layer only through its spatial average; the
ResNet-18 \texttt{layer4}/\texttt{avgpool} pair in our experiments satisfies
this condition and serves as a built-in exactness check
(Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\paragraph{Subspace alignment and classification.}
\citet{huang2016principal} prove that, for Gaussian Mixture Model
classifiers, misclassification probability is governed by the principal angles
between signal and model subspaces (product-of-sines regime for small
mismatch, sum-of-squares-of-sines for large).  This provides theoretical
grounding for why principal angles are the natural metric for classification
geometry.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Layer Representations}
\label{sec:reps}

For a network $f: \R^{H\times W\times 3}\to\R^C$ we probe a set of
checkpoints $\{a_\ell\}_\ell$ at pooling layers and fully-connected layers.
Convolutional outputs with spatial extent $H_\ell\times W_\ell$ are reduced to
channel-wise representations via global average pooling (GAP):
$h_\ell = \frac{1}{H_\ell W_\ell}\sum_{i,j}a_\ell(\cdot,i,j)\in\R^{C_\ell}$.
For the VGG-16 classifier head we probe the post-ReLU outputs of the two
hidden FC layers (denoted \texttt{fc1}, \texttt{fc2}) and the pre-softmax
logits (\texttt{out}); ResNet-18 layers are probed at stage outputs.
This gives a consistent $(N, d_\ell)$ activation matrix per layer, with
$d_\ell\in\{64,128,256,512,4096,1000\}$ depending on the layer.

\subsection{Computing TwoNN ID}

We apply the Facco CDF-fit procedure (Eq.~\eqref{eq:twonn}, with the 10\%
trimming) to each layer's $(N,d_\ell)$ activation matrix, using
scikit-learn's \texttt{NearestNeighbors} for the two-NN query.  This is the
same estimator family used by \citet{ansuini2019intrinsic}; we denote the
resulting estimate $\id_\ell$.  Error bars are the standard deviation of the
estimate over 20 random 80\% subsamples of the images.

\subsection{Computing the AGOP}
\label{sec:agop_comp}

For each mini-batch $(x,y)$ we:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Run a forward pass, retaining gradients on the intermediate output
    tensor $a_\ell$ at every probed layer.
  \item Compute $\mathcal{L} = \sum_i \ell_i$ (cross-entropy with
    \emph{sum} reduction, so each per-sample gradient carries unit weight
    regardless of batch size) and backpropagate once.
  \item Retrieve the per-sample gradients $g_{\ell,i} =
    \nabla_{a_{\ell,i}}\ell_i$.  For convolutional layers, apply spatial
    averaging: $\tilde{g}_{\ell,i} =
    \frac{1}{H_\ell W_\ell}\sum_{u,v}g_{\ell,i}(\cdot,u,v)\in\R^{C_\ell}$.
\end{enumerate}
The per-sample gradients are stored as an $(N, d_\ell)$ matrix $G_\ell$, and
$\AGOP_\ell = G_\ell^\top G_\ell / N$.  All eigendecompositions are performed
via the thin SVD of $G_\ell$ (the AGOP eigenvectors are its right singular
vectors), and retaining per-sample gradients is what enables the split-half
and within-class controls below.  All layer hooks fire in a single
forward-backward pass per batch, so the wall time is that of one training
step.

\begin{remark}
The spatial average of $\nabla_{a_\ell}\ell$ is exactly the sensitivity of
the loss to spatially-uniform per-channel perturbations of $a_\ell$, which is
the natural dual of the GAP representation.  It equals the gradient of
$\ell$ with respect to $h_\ell$ (up to the factor $H_\ell W_\ell$) whenever
downstream computation depends on $a_\ell$ only through its spatial average;
in general it coincides with the Grad-CAM weighting
\citep{selvaraju2017gradcam} and captures channel-level sensitivity while
discarding spatial structure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Alignment Metric}

Given the activation matrix $A\in\R^{N\times d}$, let $k=\max(1,\lfloor\id\rceil)$
and let $k' = \min(k, \text{rank}_{\text{eff}}(\AGOP), d-1, N-1)$ where
$\text{rank}_{\text{eff}}(\AGOP)$ is the number of AGOP eigenvalues exceeding
1\% of the maximum.  We form:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $U\in\R^{d\times k'}$: top-$k'$ PCA directions of $A$ (via truncated
    SVD of the centred activation matrix).
  \item $V\in\R^{d\times k'}$: top-$k'$ eigenvectors of $\AGOP$ (via
    \texttt{numpy.linalg.eigh}).
\end{itemize}
Alignment is then $\kcos = \frac{1}{k'}\sum_j \sigma_j(U^\top V)^2$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:alignment}).  The random baseline is $k'/d$, and we report
$\text{ratio}=\kcos/(k'/d)$.  Because the ratio divides by $k'/d$, it is not
comparable across layers of different width $d$; we therefore always report
the raw $\kcos$ alongside it, and Section~\ref{sec:results} discusses where
the two orderings disagree.  Error bars on $\kcos$ are standard deviations
over 20 random 80\% subsamples (recomputing both subspaces per subsample),
and we sweep $k$ over a grid to verify that conclusions are not artifacts of
the specific $k$ chosen by the ID estimate.

\subsection{Controls}
\label{sec:controls}

Three controls guard against the main confounds we identified.

\paragraph{Random initialisation.}  Untrained networks with the same
architecture (fixed seed) test whether alignment is training-induced or
architectural.

\paragraph{Split-half.}  Because activations and gradients of the same image
are deterministically coupled (shared ReLU masks, shared prediction error),
in-sample alignment could exceed chance without reflecting
population structure.  We therefore recompute alignment with $U$ estimated
from one stratified half of the images and $V$ from the other half
(symmetrised, 10 random splits).

\paragraph{Within-class.}  With $C$ balanced classes, both the top PCA
directions and the loss gradients are expected to contain the $C-1$
class-mean directions, so high alignment could reduce to shared class
structure.  We recompute alignment after removing the per-class mean from
both activations and per-sample gradients, with $k$ re-estimated from the ID
of the class-centred activations.  We also report the fraction of each
subspace lying in the span of the centred class means.  Because within-class
gradient residuals are functionally coupled to within-class activation
residuals of the \emph{same} samples (exactly so at the logits layer, where
the softmax Jacobian is locally affine), the within-class score must itself
be crossed with the split-half control; we report both the plain and the
crossed (within-class $\times$ split-half) versions, and treat only the
crossed version as evidence of population-level within-class alignment.

Additionally, we recompute alignment with the \emph{centred} AGOP (gradient
covariance) to check robustness to the mean-gradient direction, which is
nonzero here since the networks are not at a minimum of the evaluation loss.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We use ImageNet-pretrained VGG-16 \citep{simonyan2015vgg}
and ResNet-18 \citep{he2016resnet} from torchvision.  For controls we
re-initialise both networks with random weights (same architecture,
\texttt{torch.nn.init} defaults, fixed seed), yielding chance-level networks
on our evaluation set.

\paragraph{Data.} We use the Imagenette validation set \citep{imagenette}---a
publicly available 10-class subset of ImageNet---taking 500 balanced images
(50 per class) from the 160-pixel release, upsampled to $224\times224$ and
normalised with ImageNet statistics.  Gradients are computed using the
correct ImageNet class indices for each Imagenette class.  Two disclosures
are in order.  First, the low-resolution source degrades 1000-way top-1
accuracy to 66.2\% (VGG-16) and 65.2\% (ResNet-18) on our sample---well
below what these models achieve on native-resolution images of these
classes---so roughly a third of the gradient signal comes from
misclassified images.  Second, Imagenette's validation split is drawn
overwhelmingly ($\approx$97\%) from the original ImageNet \emph{training}
split, so the pretrained models saw most of these images (at higher
resolution) during training; the measured geometry is therefore closer to a
training-manifold than a held-out-manifold measurement.  Neither issue
affects the trained-vs-random comparison, which uses identical data for both
conditions.

\paragraph{Layers probed.}
For VGG-16: \texttt{pool1}--\texttt{pool5} (convolutional stack) and
\texttt{fc1}, \texttt{fc2}, \texttt{out} (classifier head).
For ResNet-18: \texttt{layer1}--\texttt{layer4}, \texttt{avgpool},
\texttt{fc}.

\paragraph{Implementation.} Code is available at
\url{https://github.com/kilojoules/manifold-active-subspace}.
All experiments run on a CPU/MPS device in under one hour.

\subsection{Replication: TwoNN Hunchback}

The left panels of Figure~\ref{fig:main} replicate the qualitative finding
of \citet{ansuini2019intrinsic}: ID rises through the network and falls
toward the output.  Trained VGG-16 rises from $\id=7.9\pm0.2$ at
\texttt{pool1} to $29.2\pm1.3$ at \texttt{fc1}, then falls to $13.2\pm0.5$
at the logits.  Trained ResNet-18 rises from $11.3\pm0.6$ at \texttt{layer1}
to $22.6\pm0.7$ at \texttt{layer4} and falls to $18.6\pm0.8$ at \texttt{fc}.
Random-init networks show flatter, lower profiles through the convolutional
stack; notably, their late-layer IDs (25--29 for VGG-16's FC head) are
\emph{higher} than the trained network's (13--19), so training compresses
precisely the layers whose ID \citet{ansuini2019intrinsic} link to
generalisation.  We note two protocol differences from the original study:
our representations are GAP-reduced channel vectors rather than full
activation maps, and our profile peaks at \texttt{fc1} rather than
mid-stack, so this is a qualitative rather than exact replication.

\subsection{AGOP Rank}

The centre panels show that AGOP effective rank also follows a hunchback and
is consistently larger than the TwoNN ID (e.g.\ VGG-16 rank 81 at
\texttt{pool4} vs.\ ID 19.3).  This ``rank overshooting'' indicates the
active subspace spans more directions than the dominant data-variance
subspace: the network is sensitive to perturbations in directions the data
does not strongly explore.  The threshold band in Figure~\ref{fig:main}
shows the rank ordering is stable across a $50\times$ range of eigenvalue
cutoffs.

At random init, every layer at or after the last convolutional stage has
rank ${\approx}10$--$20$ in both architectures.  This has a simple
explanation: with near-uniform softmax outputs, the logit gradient
$p - e_y$ is nearly constant within each class, so gradients concentrate in
$\approx C$ class directions ($C=10$ here), and the ranks we observe at
random-init late layers (10--21) sit exactly at this class-count floor.
Random-init VGG's \emph{early} conv layers instead show high rank
(58--133): many convolutional channels away from the head, the class-floor
structure is dispersed by the unstructured backward pass.

\paragraph{(F1) Training creates alignment on the full measure.}
Random-init networks sit at $0.8$--$1.3\times$ the random baseline at every
layer.  Trained networks exceed the baseline at every layer beyond
\texttt{pool1}/\texttt{layer1}: by $3$--$7\times$ through VGG's
convolutional stack, $35$--$43\times$ at its FC hidden layers, and
$2$--$8\times$ through ResNet.  Trained \texttt{pool1} sits at baseline
($0.9\times$): the first pooling stage shows no alignment.

\paragraph{(F2) The alignment peak depends on the metric---but the last
hidden layer peaks on both.}
On the baseline-normalised ratio, the peak is VGG-16 \texttt{fc2}
($43.4\times$); but the ratio divides by $k/d$ and therefore structurally
favours wide layers.  On raw $\kcos$, the ordering changes: ResNet-18
\texttt{layer1} (0.375) and \texttt{layer4} (0.342) exceed VGG \texttt{fc2}
(0.201).  Two statements survive both metrics: within each architecture the
last \emph{hidden} representation is a local alignment maximum
(VGG \texttt{fc2}: $\kcos=0.201$ at $d=4096$; ResNet \texttt{layer4}:
$\kcos=0.342$), and the logits layer is far below it.  These last hidden
layers are the same layers whose ID predicts generalisation in
\citet{ansuini2019intrinsic}.

\paragraph{(F3) The logits layer destroys alignment.}
Across one linear map---last hidden layer to logits---the ratio drops from
$43.4\times$ to $2.3\times$ (VGG-16) and $7.6\times$ to $2.1\times$
(ResNet-18), and raw $\kcos$ drops by $7$--$9\times$.  The AGOP subspace at
the logits also nearly leaves the class-mean span (fraction of its energy in
the span of centred class means: 0.03 at both architectures' logits, versus
0.13--0.23 at the last hidden layers).  Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
discusses this collapse.

\subsection{What the Controls Show}
\label{sec:results_controls}

\paragraph{Split-half: the alignment is population structure.}
Estimating PCA and AGOP from disjoint halves of the images
(Figure~\ref{fig:controls}, Table~\ref{tab:align}) reduces the headline
values only mildly: VGG \texttt{fc2} $43.4\!\to\!37.4\times$, \texttt{fc1}
$34.8\!\to\!28.8\times$, ResNet \texttt{layer4} $7.6\!\to\!6.6\times$.
Same-sample functional coupling between activations and gradients is not
what the measure detects.

\paragraph{Within-class: the alignment is not class-mean structure.}
Removing per-class means from both activations and per-sample gradients, and
additionally crossing with split-half, leaves VGG \texttt{fc1} at
$21.1\times$ and \texttt{fc2} at $24.6\times$ baseline, and the VGG
convolutional stack at $2.9$--$5.1\times$.  Consistently, the top-$k$ AGOP
subspace never concentrates in the 9-dimensional class-mean span (at most
32\% of its energy anywhere; 13--18\% at the headline layers).  The
manifold-gradient alignment of trained networks lives mostly in
\emph{within-class} geometry---the part of the representation that
neural-collapse analyses treat as vanishing noise.

\paragraph{A caveat the controls surfaced: architectural coupling near the
logits.}
At random init, the within-class control is far above baseline at layers
within one linear map of the logits: VGG \texttt{fc2} $16.0\times$ and
\texttt{out} $22.0\times$; ResNet \texttt{layer4} $26.6\times$ and
\texttt{fc} $38.4\times$ (crossed with split-half; Table~\ref{tab:align}).
The mechanism is linear-algebraic, not learned.  At near-uniform predictions
the softmax Jacobian is ${\approx}\frac{1}{C}(I - \frac{1}{C}\mathbf{1}
\mathbf{1}^\top)$, so within-class gradient residuals at the last hidden
layer are $W^\top J W$ applied to within-class activation residuals---a
\emph{fixed} linear map which, for wide random $W$, acts nearly
isotropically on any low-dimensional subspace and therefore maps the
activation PCA subspace approximately onto the gradient subspace.  ResNet's
residual stream keeps the end-to-end Jacobian close to such a fixed map far
into the network, which is why the coupling reaches \texttt{layer4} there
but dies two nonlinearities from the output in VGG (\texttt{fc1} at random
init: $1.0\times$).  Consequently, within-class alignment near the logits
mixes learned structure with this architectural coupling and should not be
attributed to training; the clean cells are layers at least two
nonlinearities from the output, where random init sits at
$0.7$--$1.0\times$ (VGG) and $1.5$--$1.7\times$ (ResNet) while trained
networks reach $2.9$--$5.1\times$ (VGG conv), $21$--$25\times$ (VGG FC
hidden), and $1.9$--$2.0\times$ (ResNet early stages---a marginal
separation we do not lean on).

\paragraph{Robustness.}
The centred-AGOP variant (gradient covariance rather than second moment)
matches the full measure everywhere (Figure~\ref{fig:controls}), so the
mean-gradient direction---nonzero here, since the networks are not at a
minimum of the evaluation loss---does not drive the result.  Sweeping $k$
over $[2, 50]$, the VGG FC hidden layers stay $\ge 19\times$ above baseline
for \emph{every} $k \in [5, 50]$ (peaking near $k \approx 8$--$10$ at
$67$--$122\times$), so the conclusion is insensitive to the TwoNN
finite-sample bias discussed in Section~\ref{sec:twonn}: any plausible
correction to $k$ leaves the headline finding intact.  Finally, ResNet's
\texttt{layer4} and \texttt{avgpool} probes---identical by construction
under our gradient reduction---agree to $10^{-9}$, validating the
spatial-mean pipeline where it is provably exact.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Connection to Neural Collapse}
\label{sec:nc}

Neural Collapse \citep{papyan2020prevalence,zhu2021geometric} establishes that
at the global minimiser of the unconstrained features model with balanced data,
classifier weight vectors and centred class means satisfy $W = \alpha M^\top$
(NC3, self-duality): the row space of $W$ equals the span of the centred class
means.  However, NC3 is a relationship between two linear objects and does
\emph{not} directly imply that the data manifold tangent space aligns with the
AGOP active subspace.  Alignment between those two objects requires within-class
variability (NC1) to have collapsed to zero---a condition that holds only
asymptotically under idealized training, not in finite-epoch practice.  No
existing theorem bridges NC3 to manifold-AGOP alignment.

\citet{beaglehole2024agop} provide a sharper mechanistic account of the
output-layer alignment drop: the AGOP drives layer-by-layer within-class
variability collapse (DNC1) across \emph{all} hidden layers via the right
singular structure of weight matrices, but the ETF class-mean geometry (DNC2)
emerges strongly \emph{only at the very last layer}.  The final linear
transformation thus constrains the active subspace to the $K$-dimensional
class-direction subspace, which is far narrower and differently oriented than
the broader data manifold directions captured at the penultimate layer.

Our finding that the last \emph{hidden} layer achieves the highest alignment
within each architecture, while the \emph{output} logit layer drops sharply,
is consistent with this picture.  The output layer computes $Wh$, mapping
the hidden representation through $W$; the resulting logit-space AGOP is
determined by the cross-entropy loss curvature, which depends on the current
prediction distribution rather than the data manifold geometry, yielding
generically low alignment with the data manifold---and indeed at the logits
the AGOP subspace nearly exits the class-mean span altogether (3\% energy).

The within-class control adds a sharper point.  Neural-collapse analyses
concern class means and treat within-class variability as noise that
vanishes at convergence.  Our networks are far from that regime (66\%
accuracy on the probe data), and most of the alignment we measure survives
the removal of class-mean structure: it is a relationship between
\emph{within-class} data variation and \emph{within-class} gradient
variation.  Whatever produces manifold-gradient alignment in trained
networks, it is not the NC class-mean geometry, and it is invisible to
analyses that discard within-class structure.

\subsection{Connection to Escape Dimensions}

Recent work \citep{martinelli2026escape} argues that overparameterisation
improves optimisation not by providing more ``lottery tickets''
\citep{frankle2019lottery} but by adding \emph{escape dimensions}: directions
in the loss landscape that convert sharp local minima into traversable saddle
points, making good minima more prevalent relative to bad ones.  A key
prediction is that winning subnetworks cannot be treated in isolation from the
wider network that supplied their escape-dimension context.

Our measurements offer at most an indirect connection, which we state with
care.  The escape-dimensions argument concerns the loss landscape in
\emph{parameter} space, whereas our AGOP is a first-order object (the
uncentred second moment of gradients) in \emph{activation} space.  The bridge
is that layer-$\ell$ parameter gradients factor as
$\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial W_\ell = g_\ell\, a_{\ell-1}^\top$, so the rank
of the parameter-space gradient covariance at layer $\ell$ is bounded by the
product of the activation-gradient rank and the activation-covariance rank.
Our finding that activation-space AGOP rank is moderate even at wide layers
($d=4096$) therefore implies that the parameter-space gradient also occupies
far fewer directions than the ambient dimension---consistent with a picture
in which overparameterisation supplies many more directions than the loss
actively uses.  We emphasise that our networks were not trained on the
evaluation task, so we cannot make claims about gradient concentration over
the course of training; measuring the alignment trajectory during training is
the natural follow-up.

\subsection{What the Alignment Measures---and What It Doesn't}

The alignment ratio tells us how much of the dominant data-variance
subspace is ``seen'' by the gradient, and vice versa.  A ratio of $43\times$
means that knowing which directions the data varies in is highly informative
about which directions the loss varies in---far more than chance.  But a
ratio of $43\times$ with $\kcos=0.20$ is still far from perfect alignment
($\kcos=1$): roughly $80\%$ of the gradient-subspace energy lies outside the
top-$k$ PCA subspace at the headline layer.  The controls rule out two
candidate explanations for the aligned part (same-sample coupling;
class-mean structure), but the \emph{residual misalignment} could still
reflect: (a) the approximation of curved-manifold structure by a global
linear PCA subspace---by our own Background argument, the top-$k$ PCA
directions of a curved manifold with intrinsic dimension $k$ need not
capture its tangent structure; (b) the spatial-mean reduction for
convolutional layers; (c) genuine misalignment between data directions the
task ignores and gradient directions the data does not explore.

Disentangling these requires finer tools---local tangent space estimation,
spatially-resolved AGOP for conv layers, and more architectures---which we
leave to future work.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{TwoNN finite-sample bias.}  On synthetic data of known
  dimension at $N=500$, the estimator recovers $\hat d \approx 8.8$ at true
  ID 10 and $\hat d \approx 15$ at true ID 20: it underestimates by
  20--25\% in exactly the ID range of our late layers.  Since the alignment
  ratio divides by $k/d$, an underestimated $k$ inflates the ratio.  The
  $k$-sensitivity sweep (Section~\ref{sec:results}) quantifies how
  conclusions depend on $k$; the raw $\kcos$ values do not share this
  sensitivity.

\item \textbf{Small scale.}  We study two architectures, one dataset, and
  $N=500$ images.  Subsample error bars quantify sampling variability but
  not architecture or dataset variability.

\item \textbf{Task mismatch.}  Models are trained on ImageNet (1000 classes)
  but probed on Imagenette (10 classes) at reduced resolution, with the
  provenance caveats disclosed in Section~\ref{sec:methods}.  The networks
  are far from any minimum of the evaluation loss, so terminal-phase
  phenomena (e.g.\ full neural collapse) should not be assumed to hold here.

\item \textbf{GAP approximation.}  For convolutional layers, both objects
  are reduced to channel space, discarding spatial structure.  The
  \texttt{layer4}/\texttt{avgpool} check validates the reduction where it is
  exact, but for earlier layers the spatially-resolved alignment could
  differ.

\item \textbf{Single time point.}  We measure alignment in fixed
  pretrained networks.  Training dynamics of alignment---whether it builds
  monotonically, and whether faster alignment predicts faster
  generalisation---are unexplored.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have shown that trained CNN representations are not merely compressed
(low intrinsic dimension) but partially \emph{task-aligned}: the directions
that dominate the data variance at a layer overlap the directions along
which the loss varies far more than chance predicts---$35$--$43\times$ the
random baseline at VGG-16's FC hidden layers, up to $7.6\times$ at
ResNet-18's final block, collapsing to ${\approx}2\times$ at the logits,
with random-init controls at $0.8$--$1.3\times$.  The alignment is
population structure (it survives split-half estimation) and it is not
class-mean geometry (it survives class-mean removal at $21$--$25\times$ for
the VGG FC hidden layers): most of it lives in within-class variation.  The
overlap is nonetheless partial---raw mean $\cos^2\theta$ peaks at
0.20--0.38, far from 1.

These findings suggest that the intrinsic dimension story of
\citet{ansuini2019intrinsic} is incomplete in an informative way: what
matters is not only how much the representation is compressed, but whether
its dominant directions are the ones the task uses---and that relationship
is carried by within-class geometry that neural-collapse analyses discard.
Methodologically, our controls surface a warning for this line of work:
within one linear map of the logits, architectural coupling alone produces
strong within-class ``alignment'' (up to $38\times$ at random init, reaching
deep into ResNet via its residual stream), so gradient-representation
alignment measured near the output must be checked against random-init
controls before being attributed to learning.

Future directions include: extending to more architectures, higher-resolution
data, and networks trained on the probe task itself; tracking alignment
during training; probing whether alignment at intermediate layers is
causally linked to generalisation (e.g.\ via interventions that decorrelate
the subspaces); and testing whether the within-class alignment we observe is
the trace of the AGOP-driven feature-learning mechanism of
\citet{radhakrishnan2024mechanism,beaglehole2024agop} operating far from the
collapse regime.
